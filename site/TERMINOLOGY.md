# Yoga Retreat Terminology Glossary

A friendly guide to the terms you will encounter during your retreat. No prior knowledge is expected — everyone starts somewhere, and we are glad you are here.

---

## Ananda Marga

**"The Path of Bliss" — a spiritual tradition offering a comprehensive system of meditation, yoga, and selfless service.**

Ananda Marga (from Sanskrit *ananda*, meaning bliss, and *marga*, meaning path) is a global spiritual and social movement founded in 1955 in India. It teaches that every human being has an innate desire to move toward infinite happiness, and offers practical tools — meditation, yoga, ethical living, and service to others — to support that journey. Ananda Marga welcomes people of all backgrounds, cultures, and beliefs. The philosophy is universal: that love and compassion are the foundation of a fulfilling life.

---

## Shrii Shrii Anandamurti

**The founder of Ananda Marga, a spiritual teacher also known as Baba.**

Shrii Shrii Anandamurti (1921-1990), born Prabhat Ranjan Sarkar, was an Indian philosopher, spiritual master, and prolific author. His name means "He whose manifestation is bliss," and his students lovingly call him *Baba* (beloved father). He developed a complete system of meditation and yoga, composed over 5,018 songs (Prabhat Samgiita), and wrote extensively on topics ranging from linguistics to economics to ecology. His central teaching is that the purpose of human life is to realize one's deepest nature through love, meditation, and service.

---

## Sadhana

**Spiritual practice, especially meditation.**

From the Sanskrit root *sadh*, meaning "to accomplish" or "to arrive at the goal," sadhana is your personal practice of turning inward. It can include meditation, breathing exercises, and other contemplative techniques. Think of it as a daily appointment with your deeper self — a quiet space you carve out to nurture inner peace and awareness. During the retreat, you will have several opportunities each day to practise sadhana both individually and together with the group.

---

## Panchajanya

**Pre-dawn collective spiritual practice.**

The word *panchajanya* refers to a conch shell whose sound calls people to awakening. In retreat life, Panchajanya is the early-morning gathering (typically before sunrise) for collective meditation and spiritual practice. There is something profoundly special about meditating together while the world is still quiet and the first light is just beginning to appear. It is entirely okay if early mornings are new to you — many people discover that this becomes their favourite part of the retreat.

---

## Kiirtana

**Devotional chanting and dancing — singing the name of the Supreme with joyful movement.**

Kiirtana (from Sanskrit *kiirt*, to praise or to sing aloud) is the practice of singing a simple, uplifting mantra together while moving in a flowing dance. It is not a performance — there is no right or wrong way to do it. The repetitive singing and gentle movement help quiet the mind and open the heart, naturally preparing you for deeper meditation. Many people find kiirtana to be one of the most joyful and connecting experiences of a retreat, even if they were hesitant at first.

---

## Kaoshiki

**A yogic dance for holistic well-being, developed especially for women but practised by all.**

Kaoshiki (meaning "of the inner mind" or "pertaining to the *kosha*, the layers of the mind") is a graceful, rhythmic dance introduced by Shrii Shrii Anandamurti in 1978. It involves simple lateral movements of the body set to a specific rhythm, and it benefits the body, mind, and spirit — strengthening the spine, improving digestion, and uplifting the mood. No dance experience is needed. The movements are easy to follow, and you can go at your own pace. It is often practised as a group before meditation.

---

## Tandava

**A vigorous yogic dance traditionally practised by men, expressing the spirit of fearless determination.**

Tandava is a dynamic, energetic dance rooted in ancient Indian tradition. In Ananda Marga practice, it involves strong, rhythmic jumping movements that cultivate courage, vitality, and a fearless spirit. The name comes from the cosmic dance of Lord Shiva, symbolizing the continuous rhythm of creation and transformation. It is traditionally practised by men and is a powerful way to channel energy and strengthen both body and mind before sitting for meditation.

---

## Asanas

**Yoga postures — physical poses that balance the body, calm the mind, and support meditation.**

The Sanskrit word *asana* literally means "seat" or "comfortable position." In practice, asanas are the physical yoga postures you are probably already somewhat familiar with — gentle stretches, balances, and held poses that release tension, improve flexibility, and harmonize the body's glandular system. In the Ananda Marga tradition, asanas are seen not just as exercise but as a preparation for meditation: when the body is relaxed and balanced, the mind naturally becomes still. All levels of experience are welcome, and you are always encouraged to honour your body's limits.

---

## Shavasana

**Corpse pose — a practice of deep, conscious relaxation.**

Shavasana (from Sanskrit *shava*, meaning corpse) is the deeply restful pose you take lying flat on your back at the end of an asana session. Despite its simple appearance, it is one of the most important practices: it allows the body to fully absorb the benefits of the postures and gives the nervous system a chance to reset. You simply lie still, relax every muscle, and let go. It is a small gift of complete rest in the middle of your day.

---

## Prabhat Samgiita

**"Songs of the New Dawn" — a collection of 5,018 devotional songs composed by Shrii Shrii Anandamurti.**

Prabhat Samgiita (from *prabhat*, dawn, and *samgiita*, song) are songs written between 1982 and 1990, spanning an extraordinary range of themes — devotion, nature, the seasons, human longing, social justice, and mystical experience. They are composed in multiple languages, primarily Bengali, and set to a wide variety of melodies and rhythms. During the retreat, you may hear or sing some of these songs. They carry a sweetness and depth that many people find deeply moving, even when encountering them for the first time.

---

## Sadhana Shivir

**An extended, intensive meditation session — a deep dive into practice.**

*Shivir* means "camp" or "intensive session" in Sanskrit. A Sadhana Shivir is a dedicated block of time — often several hours — set aside for sustained spiritual practice, usually combining kiirtana, meditation, and spiritual discourse. Think of it as an immersion: by giving yourself an extended, uninterrupted period of practice, you can settle into a depth that shorter sessions may not reach. It is a highlight of many retreats, and participants often describe it as profoundly refreshing.

---

## Manuvrata

**A vow of silence — a period of intentional quiet to deepen inner awareness.**

*Manu* relates to the mind, and *vrata* means a vow or sacred resolve. During Manuvrata, you choose to refrain from speaking for a set period of time. This is not about suppression but about giving your mind a rest from the constant activity of social interaction and verbal thought. Many people find that even a few hours of silence reveals a surprising inner spaciousness. It can feel unusual at first, but most participants come to treasure it as one of the most peaceful experiences of the retreat.

---

## Satsanga

**A gathering of fellow seekers — coming together in the company of truth.**

From Sanskrit *sat* (truth, that which is real) and *sanga* (company, association), satsanga is the simple but powerful practice of gathering with others on a shared spiritual path. It may involve discussion, storytelling, questions and answers, or simply being together in a contemplative atmosphere. There is an old saying that the company we keep shapes our minds — satsanga is the intentional choice to surround yourself with warmth, sincerity, and shared aspiration.

---

## Karma Yoga

**Selfless service — putting love into action without expecting anything in return.**

*Karma* means action, and *yoga* means union. Karma Yoga is the practice of serving others as a form of spiritual practice — cooking a meal for the group, tidying a shared space, helping set up for an event — with a spirit of joy and without attachment to recognition or reward. During the retreat, there will be opportunities to contribute to the community in small, practical ways. Many people find that selfless service brings a quiet, deep satisfaction that is quite different from ordinary work.

---

## Collective Meditation (20/20), (30/30)

**The numbers indicate the format: minutes of kiirtana followed by minutes of silent meditation.**

When you see a notation like "Collective Meditation (20/20)" on the retreat schedule, it simply means 20 minutes of kiirtana (devotional chanting and movement) followed by 20 minutes of silent meditation. A "30/30" session means 30 minutes of each. The kiirtana portion warms the heart and focuses the mind, creating a natural bridge into the stillness of meditation. Practising together amplifies the experience — there is a tangible sweetness in meditating as a group.

---

## Personal Lesson

**Receiving individual meditation instruction and mantra initiation from a qualified teacher.**

A Personal Lesson is a private, one-on-one session in which an acharya (spiritual teacher) introduces you to a specific meditation technique and, when appropriate, gives you a personal mantra — a sacred sound vibration used as the focus of your meditation. This is a meaningful and intimate step in one's spiritual journey. There is no prerequisite knowledge required; the teacher will guide you gently through everything. If you are curious about deepening your meditation practice, this is a wonderful opportunity.

---

## Acharya / Dada / Didi

**Spiritual teachers in the Ananda Marga tradition — monks (Dada) and nuns (Didi) who have dedicated their lives to service and spiritual guidance.**

An *acharya* (from Sanskrit *achar*, meaning conduct or practice) is "one who teaches by example." In Ananda Marga, acharyas are trained monastics who guide meditation instruction, lead spiritual gatherings, and serve communities around the world. Male acharyas are affectionately called *Dada* (elder brother) and female acharyas are called *Didi* (elder sister). These are warm, familial titles — feel free to use them. The acharyas at the retreat are here to support you, answer your questions, and help you feel at home.

---

## Dharma Chakra

**A collective meditation circle — the regular group meditation practice in Ananda Marga.**

*Dharma* means the path of righteousness or one's essential nature, and *chakra* means circle or wheel. Dharma Chakra is the formal term for a group meditation session, typically consisting of kiirtana followed by silent meditation, performed sitting together in a circle or group. It is a cornerstone of Ananda Marga community life. Meditating together creates a shared energy that supports everyone in the circle — newcomers and experienced practitioners alike. You simply sit comfortably and follow along.

---

## Sattvic

**Pure, harmonious, and balanced — describing food, environment, or qualities that support clarity and well-being.**

In yogic philosophy, all of nature expresses three fundamental qualities (*gunas*): *sattva* (clarity and harmony), *rajas* (activity and restlessness), and *tamas* (inertia and dullness). Sattvic food is vegetarian, freshly prepared, and nourishing — free from meat, fish, eggs, onion, garlic, mushrooms, and excessive spice. During the retreat, all meals will be sattvic, designed to support both your physical health and your meditation practice. Most people find that eating this way leaves them feeling light, clear, and energized.

---

*If you have any questions about these or other terms, please do not hesitate to ask any of the retreat organizers or acharyas. There are no silly questions — we are all learning together.*
